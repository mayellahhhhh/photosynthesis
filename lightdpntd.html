Light Dependent Reactions
Introduction
The light-dependent reactions are the first stage of photosynthesis where sunlight is converted into chemical energy in the form of ATP and NADPH. These reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts.
Light Absorption
Step 1

• Chlorophyll and other pigments absorb photons or light energy.

• The arrangement of these pigments in photosystems allows them to efficiently capture light.

Exciting an Electron
Step 2

• When a photon hits a chlorophyll molecule, it excites an electron, raising it to a higher energy state.

• This energy is transferred from one chlorophyll molecule to another until it reaches a special chlorophyll molecule in the reaction center, where the electron is transferred to an electron acceptor.

Electron Transport
Step 3

• The excited electron travels through the electron transport chain (ETC), a series of proteins embedded in the thylakoid membrane.

• As the electron moves along the chain, it releases energy used to pump hydrogen ions (H+) from the stroma into the thylakoid lumen, creating a proton gradient.

ATP Synthesis
Step 4

• The high concentration of H+ ions in the thylakoid lumen creates potential energy.

• H+ ions flow back into the stroma through ATP synthase, a protein that synthesizes ATP from ADP and inorganic phosphate (Pi) as the ions pass through.

NADPH Formation
Step 5

• After exiting the ETC, the electron enters a second photosystem (Photosystem I) where it gets re-energized by another photon.

• The energized electron moves through another electron transport chain and ultimately reduces NADP+ to form NADPH, which carries energy and electrons to the Calvin Cycle.

Importance of Light Dependent Reactions

• These reactions are essential for converting solar energy into chemical energy, providing ATP and NADPH, which fuel the subsequent Calvin Cycle.

• A byproduct of water splitting during these reactions is oxygen, which is vital for aerobic life on Earth.

Factors Affecting Light Dependent Reactions

• Greater light intensity increases the rate of electron excitation, enhancing ATP and NADPH production.

• Sufficient water is necessary for the photolysis (splitting of water) process, which generates electrons and protons for the reactions.

• Optimal temperatures are required for enzyme activity involved in the reactions; too high or too low can reduce efficiency.
