Calvin Cycle
Introduction
The Calvin Cycle is the second stage of photosynthesis, using ATP and NADPH from the light-dependent reactions to convert carbon dioxide into glucose. It takes place in the stroma of chloroplasts.
Carbon Fixation
Phase 1

• Carbon dioxide from the atmosphere enters the stroma of the chloroplast.

• The enzyme ribulose bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase (rubisco) catalyzes the reaction between carbon dioxide and ribulose bisphosphate (RuBP), forming a six-carbon intermediate that splits into two molecules of 3-phosphoglycerate (3-PGA).

Reduction
Phase 2

• Each 3-PGA molecule is phosphorylated by ATP, converting it into 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate (1,3-BPG).

• Then, NADPH donates electrons to reduce 1,3-BPG to glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate (G3P). During this process, a phosphate group is released.

RuBP Regeneration
Phase 3

• For every six G3P molecules produced, five are used to regenerate RuBP, allowing the cycle to continue.

• ATP is utilized to phosphorylate G3P back into RuBP, ensuring that the cycle can repeat.

• One G3P molecule leaves the cycle to contribute to the formation of glucose and other carbohydrates.

Importance of the Calvin Cycle

• The Calvin Cycle is crucial for converting inorganic carbon (CO₂) into organic molecules (sugars), providing energy for plants and, indirectly, for all other life forms.

• The sugars produced (like glucose) serve as building blocks for more complex carbohydrates, such as starch and cellulose, which are vital for plant structure and energy storage.

Factors Affecting the Calvin Cycle

• While the Calvin Cycle itself does not require light, it depends on the ATP and NADPH generated from the light-dependent reactions, making light availability crucial.

• Higher CO₂ levels can enhance the rate of photosynthesis, as more carbon is available for fixation.

• The Calvin Cycle is sensitive to temperature changes; extreme temperatures can affect enzyme activity and overall efficiency.
